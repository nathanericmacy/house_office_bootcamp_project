Overview:
I set out to evaluate legislative effectiveness of freshman members in relation to their office location for the U.S. House of Representatives. Freshman members of Congress enter a lottery drawing to receive the order in which they get to pick their offices. The results of this lottery are not publicly available, so at this time, I was not able to compare drawing order and legislative effectiveness. I have been in contact with my representative (Julie Johnson) and the House Committee on Administration to possibly getting that data. I used a metric created by the Center for Effective Lawmaking (CEL), LES 1.0, to measure legislative effectiveness and a seniority score, called “Benchmark.” Office locations were taken from the House website using the internet archive (wayback machine).

Findings: 
There is a negative correlation between floor number and LES 1.0 for Freshman Members of Congress in the Cannon House Office Building. Meaning that being on the first floor resulted in a more effective member. Longworth had the opposite result. Rayburn does not have enough freshman members to reach a concussion.
It is not statistically significantly supported that the average LES 1.0 scores for the two members surrounding a freshman member of Congress are related to the freshman member's LES for that term. In fact, there is a slight negative correlation. The same is true for comparing the neighbors' seniority (Benchmark) as well.
